Building on this foundation, I researched and compared the license and support costs for Windows, macOS, and Linux. This activity allowed me to look at operating systems not just from a technical perspective, but also from a financial and strategic one. I learned that Windows requires a paid license, usually costing between $139 and $199, with additional expenses often incurred for enterprise-level support. In contrast, macOS is provided at no extra cost with Apple hardware, but users who want extended coverage and technical support can purchase AppleCare plans. Linux stands out because many community distributions are free to use, yet enterprise options such as Red Hat, SUSE, and Ubuntu Pro rely on paid subscriptions for professional support and security updates. This comparison showed me that choosing an operating system is not just about functionality but also about long-term budgeting and support needs. I can apply this knowledge when planning IT expenses, selecting the right platforms for projects, or advising teams and organizations on how to balance cost with performance.

Finally, I researched the components required to set up a home network. I learned that a basic setup requires an internet service provider connection, a modem to translate the signal, and a router to manage traffic and enable both wired and wireless connections. To enhance performance, Ethernet cables provide stable wired links, while switches expand the number of devices that can connect. Wireless access points or mesh systems can also improve coverage across larger spaces.
